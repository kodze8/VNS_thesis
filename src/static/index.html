Variable Neighbourhood Search

An explainable visualisation of the search

What this shows

The Travelling Salesman Problem asks for the shortest tour that visits every city once and returns to the start. Variable Neighbourhood Search (VNS) looks for a good tour by repeating two steps: it shakes the current tour with a random move to escape where it is stuck, then runs a 2-opt local search to tidy the result back up. If the new tour is shorter it is kept; if not, VNS tries a stronger shake next time. The shakes are ordered from gentlest to strongest:

N1 Or-opt 1 — move 1 city (small)
N2 Or-opt 2 — move 2 cities
N3 Or-opt 3 — move 3 cities
N4 double bridge — 4 edges (large)
N5 double double bridge — up to 8 edges

Pick an instance and configuration, press Run to search live, then use the slider to step through the search. Each panel below explains what it is showing.

The tour the search is trying at this step. Coloured edges were added by this step's shake, coloured by the level (N1–N5) that produced them; dashed grey edges were removed. Grey dots are the cities.

Cost trajectory

The best tour cost so far (black) and the current candidate cost (grey) at each iteration. Dots mark accepted improvements, coloured by the level that produced them. The green dashed line is the known optimum.
Neighborhood statistics

Times used per neighborhood

How often each shake level was applied over the whole run. Usage is fairly even because the search keeps cycling back to the gentle shakes.

Contribution rate per neighborhood (%)

The share of each level's uses that led to an accepted improvement. The gentle shakes (N1) tend to pay off most often per use; the strongest (N5) rarely does, but it is the only one that can escape the hardest local optima.
Shaking dynamics

Mean shake delta vs LS recovery

For each level, the average cost a shake adds (disruption, black) next to how much 2-opt removes again (recovery, grey). The two bars stay close at every level — 2-opt undoes most of any shake, which is what lets the search wander without drifting far from a good tour.

Improvements over iterations

The running total of accepted improvements. It rises steeply early on and then flattens, showing the search finds its best tour early and spends the rest of the run without improving.
